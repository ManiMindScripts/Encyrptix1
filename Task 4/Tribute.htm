Dr Abdul Qadeer khan

1936-2021

MOHSIN-E-PAKISTAN

Dr. Abdul Qadeer Khan , often referred to as the "father of Pakistan's nuclear bomb," played a pivotal role in transforming Pakistan into a nuclear-armed state. Born on April 1, 1936, in Bhopal, India, Khan moved to Pakistan after the partition in 1947. He pursued advanced studies in metallurgy and nuclear engineering in Europe, earning a doctorate from the Catholic University of Leuven in Belgium. Upon returning to Pakistan in the mid-1970s, Khan established the Kahuta Research Laboratories (now Khan Research Laboratories), where he spearheaded the development of uranium enrichment technology. This breakthrough enabled Pakistan to conduct its first successful nuclear tests in 1998, solidifying its status as a nuclear power. While hailed as a national hero in Pakistan for his contributions to the country’s security, Khan's legacy is also marred by controversy due to his involvement in nuclear proliferation activities, including allegations of sharing nuclear technology with Iran, North Korea, and Libya. Despite these controversies, Dr. A.Q. Khan remains a highly influential and complex figure in the history of global nuclear proliferation and Pakistan's national defense. He passed away on October 10, 2021, leaving behind a legacy that continues to evoke both admiration and debate.

Biographies

  • Worked at the Physical Dynamics Research Laboratory in Amsterdam, Netherlands.
  • Established and led the Kahuta Research Laboratories (KRL) in 1976.
  • Played a critical role in developing Pakistan's uranium enrichment capabilities.
  • In 2004, Khan was implicated in a global nuclear proliferation network.
  • He remained a popular figure in Pakistan, receiving several national awards, including the Nishan-e-Imtiaz, the country's highest civilian honor, in 1996 and 1999.
  • Dr. A.Q. Khan’s contributions to Pakistan’s nuclear program are seen as pivotal in maintaining a strategic balance in South Asia.
